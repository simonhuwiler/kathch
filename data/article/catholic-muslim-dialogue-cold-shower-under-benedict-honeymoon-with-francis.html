Catholic-Muslim dialogue: Cold shower under Benedict, honeymoon with Francis

The Swiss Capuchin Bishop Paul Hinder (80) will receive Pope Francis in Bahrain: «It’s about theological dialogue, but also about practical peace work, justice and care for the common house.» An interview about Benedict’s Regensburg speech, the King of Bahrain – and the World Cup in Qatar.

By Raphael Rauch, kath.ch

Bishop Paul, where have I caught you right now?

Bishop Paul Hinder*: For once, I am in Abu Dhabi at the moment. In the last few weeks I have been travelling a lot: in Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Oman – and even in Berlin.

What did you do in Berlin?

Hinder: The Catholic Foreign Secretariat of the German Bishops’ Conference invited me. It was about a colloquium with aspiring German diplomats. As part of their attaché training, they are also required to get to know the church players in foreign policy. I was able to report on my work in the Arab world.

At 80, you’re busier than some Swiss bishops. Bishop Valerio Lazzeri resigned at 59. Are you doing something wrong?

Hinder: Bishop Valerio did not take the decision lightly. I wish him much strength for his new phase of life. And I too hope that I will soon be able to return to Switzerland. It is quite unlikely that the pope will already announce in Bahrain who will be my successor for the Northern Vicariate.

You already welcomed Pope Francis in Abu Dhabi in 2019. Have papal visits become routine for you? 

Hinder: That would be an exaggeration (laughs). I received a call from the nuncio on the first day of my holiday. Under the seal of secrecy, I learned that the pope wanted to travel to Bahrain. To be honest, it wrecked my holiday because I knew how much work we would face.

How many hours sleep are you currently getting? 

Hinder: A good five and a half hours, as usual. At 10 p.m. I turn off the light – and then I sleep until 3.40 a.m.. In the afternoon I need a little siesta.

Why is Bahrain important to Pope Francis?

Hinder: The pope is committed to building more bridges to the Islamic world. It’s about theological dialogue, but also about practical peace work, justice and care for the common house. 

Europe fears an energy crisis. How are you experiencing this in Bahrain, where the oil is gushing?

Hinder: Bahrain is of course profiting from rising energy prices. These bring additional money into the coffers. But here, too, people are thinking about the future: alternative energies are being developed, such as solar power. The war in Ukraine feels far away but Bahrain is looking to align itself with the US.

When you think about the Catholic-Muslim dialogue: are we experiencing a honeymoon now after Benedict’s Regensburg speech in 2006 was a low point?

Hinder: The Regensburg speech was a cold shower. I’ve been in Abu Dhabi since 2004 and it was immediately clear to me that there would be trouble! During the Regensburg speech, I had appointments in Rome. Immediately after my return to the Emirates, I was summoned to see the Emir’s advisor. I was asked to explain the Catholic Church’s position on Islam. It was a delicate appointment – but I was able to make it clear to the advisor that Pope Benedict is a friend of Islam, he just expressed himself very clumsily and deeply regrets that. The speech was not a diplomatic masterpiece – I’m sure that Benedict sees it that way, too.

And then?

Hinder: The saying « a crisis is an opportunity» came true. The Holy See was very nervous. It feared that relations with the Islamic world could deteriorate massively. That’s why it did everything it could to ramp up the Catholic-Islamic dialogue. This gave the dialogue a new momentum. In the words of St. Paul: Blessed guilt, to have produced such an outcome!

Are we experiencing a honeymoon with Francis? 

Hinder: Pope Francis is very well received in the Arab world with his open, warm manner. He is held in high esteem by everyone. He is considered, much more than Benedict, a true friend of Islam because he works intensively with the Grand Imam Ahmad al-Tayyib. The trips to Abu Dhabi, Morocco, Iraq, Kazakhstan and now Bahrain illustrate how important Islam is to Francis. The Arabs are also proud that the pope appointed a Muslim as a member of the papal bioethics academy. 

The Islamic world is big. Why is Francis travelling to Bahrain of all places?

Hinder: The King of Bahrain has made dialogue with the Holy See a top priority. He already asked my predecessor to move the bishop’s seat to Bahrain. My predecessor cleverly countered: « We need a cathedral in Bahrain for that.» And the king said, « No problem, I’ll give you the land. » The king holds Pope Francis in high esteem. And the Holy See appreciates that Bahrain is a moderate player in the Middle East. It’s a little dwarf, wedged between Saudi Arabia and Qatar – and the mullahs in Iran are on the far shore. Pope Francis’ trip to Bahrain is a signal to powers in the region of how much the Vatican appreciates Bahrain’s moderate politics.

The journey has a biblical motto: «Peace on earth to men of good will.» What does this motto signify?

Hinder: We proposed this motto because there is war in the world – in Yemen, in Ukraine, everywhere in the world. Pope Francis stated how important peace is to him in his encyclical 

«Fratelli tutti.» We think the motto also fits very well in terms of the timing. In four weeks, we will have the first of Advent. The motto of the pope’s journey is the gloria of a Christmas hymn. With the Incarnation of God, peace is promised to us. But we have to do something to achieve it.

Why aren’t all people of good will? Why are there dictators like Putin?

Hinder: I don’t have an answer to that. I can’t look into Putin’s heart. But I don’t want to deny that Putin also has good sides deep in his heart. That makes it all the more important to hope for these good sides. Ultimately, it remains a mystery why people behave the way they do. 

How does Bahrain stage a mass event in the age of Covid?

Hinder: We have a strict safety concept. Everyone who’s in the pope’s immediate vicinity must present a PCR test. We must not endanger the pope’s health. I don’t know the details, but I assume that the faithful will wear masks. In Abu Dhabi, we still wear masks in church services.

Where will the pope stay?

Hinder: In the guest house of the royal palace!

Meaning in very grand surroundings!

Hinder: Very grand by Swiss standards. For the Gulf States, that’s normal (laughs).

Who is paying for the pope’s visit?

Hinder: It’s an official state visit. That means that the King of Bahrain is paying for almost everything. I’m very happy about that, because we couldn’t afford this mega-event. Bahrain is also responsible for the Holy Father’s security – in cooperation with the Gendarmerie, meaning the Vatican police, and the Swiss Guard.

The cathedral of Bahrain is called «Our Lady of Arabia.» Is this an inculturation of Mary?

Hinder: No, rather the opposite of inculturation! The statue that stands in Bahrain is a copy of «Our Lady of Mount Carmel». 

How did that come about?

Hinder: The Carmelites used to run he vicariate in Kuwait. Shortly before the Second World War, they brought the icon to Kuwait and distributed it there. This is how «Our Lady of Mount Carmel» became the icon «Our Lady of Arabia.» But it was only really distributed widely in Kuwait. My predecessor, Bishop Camillo Ballin, asked me if we could not extend the veneration to the whole peninsula. I agreed. With the inauguration of the cathedral, it has now also become popular in Bahrain. Things can grow quite a bit – also in Saudi Arabia. Many faithful from Saudi Arabia come over the bridge to Bahrain to worship here.

If «Our Lady of Mount Carmel» has problems gaining acceptance: who is the favourite with your faithful?

Hinder: All our churches have a grotto. And the Virgin of Lourdes is number one. Sometimes there’s also the Virgin of Fatima. As we have many people from India, Mother Teresa is of course also very popular. And Padre Pio.

You mentioned Saudi Arabia. Have you ever had an audience with MbS, Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman?

Hinder: No. I am too small a light for that (laughs). Unfortunately, we are only making very modest progress in Saudi Arabia. Compared to the past, however, tolerance has grown. The power of the religious police was broken ten years ago. Since then, things have been a little better. But dialogue with Riyadh is still very delicate. Especially since certain evangelical groups don’t play by the rules. They have no understanding of the fact that Saudi Arabia does not tolerate missionary work or proselytism. Unfortunately, we’re also being punished for the misconduct of some free churches – even though we obediently play by the rules.

Is the current protest in Iran a topic in Bahrain?

Hinder: There are images of the protests in the newspapers. The Sunnis look at Iran with schadenfreude. On the other hand, they know very well that they have to overcome similar problems at home.

What are the faithful hoping for from the pope’s visit?

Hinder: Assurance, courage and hope. Due to the World Cup, everyone is now reporting about the poor working conditions in Qatar. But I don’t have the feeling that things look any better in the other countries of the Middle East. Most guest workers from the Philippines or India are precariously employed. If they lose their jobs, they have to leave the country immediately. So they are vulnerable to extortion and can be exploited.

Are you involved with the World Cup in Qatar?

Hinder: Only indirectly. Our church in Doha is located near one of the stadiums. We don’t know yet whether the faithful will still be able to attend services or whether everything will be cordoned off. I’m not a football fan myself, but of course many of the priests and the faithful are passionate about football. They can hardly wait for it to start. One active youth movement wants to get involved during the World Cup.

What’s this youth movement all about?

Hinder: It’s a catholic youth movement that originated in Kerala, India, and is now present in various countries around the world. The young people want the Catholic Church to be present at the World Cup. The church should be presented to fans in an attractive way.

Gianni Infantino of Switzerland is the boss of Fifa. Did he invite you to the opening match?

Hinder: Not so far (laughs). I probably don’t exist as far as he’s concerned.

* Bishop Paul Hinder (80) grew up in Thurgau. The Capuchin has lived in Abu Dhabi since 2004, first as auxiliary bishop and then as bishop. During his episcopate he was responsible for both the southern and northern vicariates of Arabia. 

In May, Pope Francis accepted Hinder’s resignation due to his age. There is now a successor for the Apostolic Vicariate of Southern Arabia: the Italian Capuchin Paolo Martinelli. Bishop Paul Hinder remains responsible for the northern vicariate – as Apostolic Administrator. In that capacity he is the host of Pope Francis who is due to visit Bahrain from November 3 to 6.
