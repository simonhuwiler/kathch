Cardinal Kurt Koch criticises Patriarch Kirill: «Such behaviour must shock every ecumenical heart»

Cardinal Kurt Koch (72) has defended the Vatican’s diplomacy in an interview with Swiss catholic website kath.ch (Saturday). He said the key to peace in Ukraine lay in Moscow. That was why Pope Francis was keeping «all doors open.» Koch said he was shocked that Patriarch Kirill was lending religious legitimacy to the war.

Raphael Rauch, kath.ch

The Holy See and Switzerland are intensifying their cooperation. How do you perceive your home country in the Ukraine war?

Cardinal Kurt Koch*: We are experiencing a new situation. Switzerland has started a new debate about the question of neutrality. President Ignazio Cassis attended a peace demonstration in Bern that President Volodymyr Zelensky was connected to by video link. National Council President Irène Kaelin travelled to Ukraine. She explained her trip by saying: we must stand on the side of international law. Federal Councillors already emphasised in the past that neutrality and solidarity belong together. I think this is right. 

Is Pope Francis a Putin sympathiser?

Koch: Pope Francis wants to end the war – also by leaving all doors open to achieve this goal. Slamming the door on protagonists who hold the key to peace will not bring you closer to peace. The Russian president has visited the Pope twice in Rome. Immediately after the war began, the Pope went to the Russian embassy at the Vatican. No other head of state does that. Usually the ambassadors are summoned. The Pope is doing everything in his power to work towards peace.

You were present at the video conference when the Pope spoke with Moscow Patriarch Kirill and Metropolitan Hilarion. How did you find the conversation?

Koch: The Pope let the Patriarch speak first. He gave a comprehensive account of his view of the causes of the war. The Pope did not respond to this political view but he made clear: we are not clerics of the state, we are shepherds for the people – and together we must find solutions for peace. 

You told Cologne’s Domradio that you are not sure whether this message got through to the Patriarch.

Koch: What I meant was: I cannot see into the heart of the Patriarch. I can only see how he reacts.

How does it feel to take part in a video conference that could make world history – but you can’t convince the other person?

Koch: The war in Ukraine is terrible and, as the Pope has repeatedly stressed, absurd. And it is fatal for so many people. Everything must therefore be done to end this terrible and senseless war. Even if one has to accept setbacks in diplomatic relations in this endeavour, one must keep at it.

One of your highlights as cardinal was the meeting of Francis and Kirill in Havana and the signing of the Havana Declaration. Do we now have to reassess the significance of this encounter?

Koch: I do not count the Havana meeting among my achievements. Without the initiative and the will of Patriarch Kirill, this meeting would not have taken place. Today, relations have taken on a completely different character. That is because when such a terrible war is even given religious legitimacy, such behaviour must shock every ecumenical heart. I am therefore glad that Pope Francis has cancelled the meeting with Patriarch Kirill scheduled for June. It would probably have been widely misunderstood. Nevertheless, the dialogue must continue.

When will the Pope travel to Moscow to meet Putin?

Koch: So far, the Pope has not received an answer from the Russian President to his request.

What does Metropolitan Hilarion, the Moscow Patriarchate’s foreign representative, say?

Koch: After the Zoom meeting, I only informed the Metropolitan of the cancellation of the meeting in June. No further talks have taken place.

Last week you had the plenary of the Pontifical Council for Promoting Christian Unity. What impetus came from Ukraine in your view? 

Koch: We had a long Zoom meeting with Archbishop Sviatoslav Shevchuk who gave us an in-depth account of the situation in Ukraine. This hybrid meeting was very moving. He also told us that his name had been at the top of the list of people to be killed. But he lives in great confidence. I experience the Greek Catholic Church in Ukraine as a very devout community that draws hope from faith even during these dramatic events.

The former abbot of Einsiedeln, Martin Werlen, has publicly asked the question: when are you travelling to Kyiv?

Koch: As far as I know, he did not ask, but called on the Pope to send me to Kyiv as well. My answer: if my coming to Ukraine is desired and the Pope agrees, I will travel to Kyiv. But it is the Pope who decides, not Father Werlen. Moreover, I do not share his opinion that they would listen to me in Moscow when they do not even listen to the Pope there.

* Swiss Cardinal Kurt Koch (72) is President of the Pontifical Council for Promoting Christian Unity, which makes him the Pope’s minister for ecumenical affairs.
